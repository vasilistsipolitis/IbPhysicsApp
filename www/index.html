Definitions by Chapter

Fundamental units
Kilogram (kg), metre (m), second (s), kelvin (K), mole (mol), ampere (A), and candela (cd).
Random error
An error due to inexperience of the observer and the difficulty of reading instruments. It can be reduced by repeated measurements.
Systematic error
An error due to incorrectly calibrated instruments – it is the same for all data points and cannot be reduced by repeated measurements.
Scalar
A physical quantity with magnitude only.
Vector
A physical quantity with magnitude and direction.

Acceleration
Rate of change of velocity with time.
Centripetal acceleration
The acceleration due to a changing velocity direction. It points toward the centre of the circular path and equals (v^2)/r.
Centripetal force
The name of any force or resultant of individual forces that points towards the centre of a circular path.
Displacement
The displacement in a given direction from a fixed origin. It is a vector.
Efficiency
The ratio of useful output work (or power) to input work (or power).
Energy conservation
Energy cannot be destroyed or created. It can be transformed from one form into another.
Equipotential surface
The set of points that have the same gravitational potential.
Escape speed
Speed of object at Earth’s surface so that it will escape from the gravitational field / travel to infinity.
Gravitational field strength
The force per unit mass exerted on a point/small mass.
Gravitational potential
The work done per unit mass in bringing a small/point mass from infinity to the point (in the gravitational field).
Gravitational potential energy
The work done to move a body from infinity to a point in a gravitational field.
Hooke's Law
The tension in a spring is proportional to its extension and opposite to it.
Impulse
The total change in the momentum of a system as a result of a force acting on it.
Law of gravitation
There is an attractive force between any two points masses.
Momentum conservation
If the net external force on a system is zero, the total momentum of the system stays the same.
Newton's 1st Law
An object will remain in a constant velocity (or at rest) unless a resultant external force acts upon it.
Newton's 2nd Law
The rate of change of momentum of a body is proportional to the net force acting on it.
Newton's 3rd Law
When two bodies A and B interact the force that A exerts on B is equal and opposite to the force that B exerts on A.
Orbit
The path of an object when under the influence of just the force of gravitation.
Orbital speed
The speed of a body in an orbit.
Power
The rate at which energy is transferred (or the rate at which work is done).
Translational equilibrium
The state of a system where the net external force is zero.
Velocity
The rate of change with time of the displacement.
Weight
The force of gravitational attraction on a body.
Work
The product of the force times the distance moved by its point of application in the direction of the force.

Adiabatic
A thermodynamic process in which no thermal energy is exchanged. (Q = 0)
Avagadro constant
The number of molecules in one mole of a substance. (6.02 x 10^23)
Boyle's Law
At constant temperature, a fixed quantity of an ideal gas obeys pV = constant.
Entropy
A measure of the disoder of a thermodynamic system.
1st Law of thermodynamics
The thermal energy, Q supplied to a system equals the change in internal energy, ΔU plus any work done, W. (Q=ΔU+W)
Heat (thermal) capacity
The energy/heat required to raise/change the temperature of a substance by 1 K/°C.
Ideal gas
A gas with no intermolecular force that obeys the ideal gas law, PV = nRT at all temperatures, pressures and volumes.
Internal energy
The total (sum of) kinetic and potential energy of the molecules of a substance.
Isothermal
A thermodynamic process in which the temperature stays the same (remains constant).
Pressure
The force normal to an area per unit area.
Pressure-temperature law
At constant volume, a fixed quantity of an ideal gas obeys P/T = constant.
2nd Law of Thermodynamics
The entropy of an isolated system (a system on which no work is being done from the outside) never decreases.
Specific heat capacity
The amount of thermal energy required to raise the temperature of unit mass by 1 K.
Specific latent heat of fusion/vaporization
The energy required to melt/vaporize a unit mass at constant temperature.
Temperature
A measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules of a substance. It's also the direction of the flow of thermal energy (heat) from high to low temperature/
Volume-temperature law
At constant pressure, a fixed quantity of an ideal gas obeys V/T = constant.
Work
The work done by an ideal gas equals the area under the graph of pressure verses volume. (W = PΔV)

Amplitude
Magnitude of the maximum displacement from the equilibrium position. (The square of the amplitude is proportional to the energy carried by the wave (per unit length/area of the wavefront).
Brewster angle
The angle of incidence for which the reflected light is 100% plane-polarized parallel to the reflecting surface.
Brewster's law
The Brewster angle θa is given by tan θa = n1/n2 when light from a medium with refractive index n1 is incident on a medium with refractive index n2.
Coherent
Two sources of identical waves are in constant phase difference between them.

Electrical conductor
The material that allows the flow of charge through it.
Electrical insulator
The material that does not allow the flow of charge through it.
Law of conservation of electric charge
The total charge of an isolated system cannot change. This means that the total charge before any process should be equal to the total charge afterwards.
Coulomb’s Law
The electrostatic force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the two charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.
Electric field strength
It is defined as the force per unit charge experience by a small positive test charge.
Electric potential difference
The work done per unit charge to move a small positive test charge between two points.
Electronvolt
The work done when a charge equals to one electron charge is taken across a potential difference of 1V.
Electric current
The rate of flow of electrical charge.
Resistance
The ratio of potential difference to the current.
Ohm’s law
The current through the conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided the temperature remains constant.
Electromotive force
It is the electrical power supplied by the source per unit current delivered by the source.
Ideal ammeter
The ammeter that has zero resistance.
Ideal voltmeter
The voltmeter that has infinite resistance.
LDR
Increase in light intensity, decrease in resistance of LDR.
Thermistor
Increase in temperature, decrease in resistance of thermistor.
Strain gauge
Increase in length, increase in resistance.
In a magnetic field
F on moving charge is always at right angle to velocity=>circular motion=>Fcircular=Fmagnetic. If charge enters field at an angle, the path will be a spiral one.

Uniform circular motion
Motion in a circle of constant radius at constant speed.
Angular displacement
Angle through which the object moves (when in circular motion).
Centripental force
Net force acting on an object experiencing acceleration a. Provided by friction, gravity, tension, normal force or others.
Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation
Every point particle in the universe attracts every other point particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Only for point masses that have equal and opposite forces, they are always attractive and act between all objects in the universe. (F=GMm/r^2)
Gravitational field strength
It is defined as the force per unit mass exerted on a test mass placed at that point. (We assume that all masses are concentrated at its centre.)
Acceleration due to gravity
It varies due to altitude, local geology and shape of the earth (it is not absolutely spherical).
Neutral point
The point between two masses which net gravity is zero.

Nucleons
Protons or neutrons.
Isotopes
Nuclides of the same element with the same proton number but different neutron number.
Coulomb interaction
Repulsive force arising from the protons with the atom being all positive in charge. (long-ranged)
Strong nuclear force/strong interaction
Strong FOA between hadrons that can overcome coulomb interaction. (experienced by both protons and neutrons, short-ranged)
Radioactive half-life
The time taken for half the unstable nuclei in a given sample to decay.
Unified atomic mass
1/12 the mass of a Carbon-12 atom.
Mass detect
The difference between the total mass of the constituents of a nucleus and the mass of the nucleus.
Binding energy
Amount of energy that is released when a nucleus is assembled from its component nucleons.
Binding energy per nucleon
Amount of energy that is released when a nucleus is assembled from its component nucleons divided by the number of nucleons making up the nucleus.

Modulation
Intensities of the maxima of the double slit pattern is enveloped and "bounded" by the intensity distribution of the single slit pattern.
Resolution
Minimum distance between distinguisable objects in an image.
Angular resolution
Smallest angular distance between two light sources that can be resolved.
Rayleigh Criterion
Two sources are just resolvable when the first minimum of the diffraction pattern of one of the sources falls on the central maximum of the diffraction pattern of the other source.
Resolvance or Diffraction grating
Ration of the wavelength λ of the light to the smallest difference in wavelength that can be resolved by the diffraction grating Δλ.
Dopple effect
Change in the measured frequency of a wave that results from the relative motion of a source and/or an observer relative to the medium in which the wave is propagated.

Gravitational Potential Energy
Work done by an external force in moving it from infinity to that point. (ΔGPE=Kinetic Energy=>Vesc).
Gravitational Potential
Work done per unit mass by an external force to bring a test mass from infinity to that point.
Electric potential
Work done per unit charge in moving a small positive charge from infinity to that point.

Absolute magnitude
A measure of how bright an object appears from a distance of 10pc (apparent magnitude).
Apparent brightness
A measure of the brightness of a star as it appears from Earth. (Power/rate of energy received per unit area at Earth).
Apparent magnitude
A measure of the brightness of a star as it appears from Earth in a relative classification/logarithmic scale.
Binary star system
Two stars orbiting a common centre.
Cepheid variable
A star whose luminosity changes periodically due to contractions and expansions of its surface.
Chandrasekhar limit
The largest mass a white dwarf can have. It is about 1.4 solar masses (1.4M)
Constellation
A collection of stars that form a recognizable pattern as viewed from Earth that look close together.
Cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB)
An electromagnetic radiation in the microwave-region that fills in the universe. It has a blackbody spectrum corresponding to a temperature of about 2.7K. It’s also the remnant of the high temperature at time of the Big Bang and provides one of the strong pieces of evidence for the model.
Critical density
The density at which universe will expand forever but rate of expansion will approach zero / the density at which the universe will begin to contract after infinite amount of time.
Galaxy
A collection of 100 billion stars.
Hubble’s law
Distant galaxies are moving away from Earth with speed v that is proportional to their distance d from Earth. (v = Hd) H is Hubble’s constant.
Main sequence
Star undergoing nuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium. They lie on a strip on the HR diagram from top left to bottom right.
Neutron star
An end stage in the evolution of high-mass stars. A collapsed star compose almost entirely of neutrons whose degeneracy pressure balance inward pressure due to gravity. It is very dense and often has a very strong magnetic field and rotates.
Olber’s paradox
The night sky would be bright if there were an infinite number of stars in an eternal universe.
Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit
The largest mass a neutron star can have. It is about 2-3 solar masses. The uncertainty in this limit comes from the fact that the equation of state of the matter inside a neutron star is not precisely known.
Parallax method
A method for measuring the distances to nearby stars that relies on the fact that star appears displaced relative to the background of distant stars when viewed from two different positions in space.
Planetary nebula
The ejection of mass from an exploding red giant star.
Pulsars
Rotating neutron stars emitting radio waves.
Spectral class
A classification of stars according to surface temperature and colour. The classes are OBAFGKM.
Spectroscopic parallax
A method for measuring the distance to a main-sequence star.
Stellar evolution
The evolution of a star from its birth to its life on the main sequence.
Supernova
The ejection of mass from an exploding supergiant star.
White dwarf
An end stage in the evolution of low-mass stars. It is a stable star in which the degeneracy pressure of electrons balances the inward pressure due to gravity.
Wien displacement law
The wavelength at which most of the energy from a star is emitted is related to surface temperature through. (λT = 2.90 x 10^-3 Km)
